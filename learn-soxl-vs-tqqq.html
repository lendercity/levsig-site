SOXL vs TQQQ — Which Leveraged ETF Should You Trade?

LevSig · July 2026 · 5 min read

levsig.com → Learn → SOXL vs TQQQ

SOXL and TQQQ are both 3x leveraged ETFs. Both are widely traded. Both are in the LevSig signal universe. But they are fundamentally different instruments — one tracks the Nasdaq-100, the other tracks semiconductors alone. Understanding that difference is essential before trading either systematically.

The Basics

SOXL tracks the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOXX) — 30 semiconductor companies including NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom, TSMC, and Intel. TQQQ tracks the Nasdaq-100 — 100 of the largest non-financial companies on the Nasdaq, heavily weighted toward tech but including Amazon, Meta, Costco, and others.

Volatility: The Biggest Difference

This is where SOXL and TQQQ diverge most dramatically. Semiconductors are one of the most volatile sectors in the market — highly sensitive to earnings, supply chain disruptions, AI demand cycles, geopolitical risk (Taiwan, China export controls), and macro interest rate moves.

SOXL routinely moves 5-10% in a single session during high-volatility periods. TQQQ's typical daily range is 3-6%. At 3x leverage, SOXL can move 15-30% in a week during a strong trend or a sharp correction — a magnitude that few retail traders are psychologically prepared for.

Position sizing matters more with SOXL. The higher volatility means ATR-based stops are wider in dollar terms, and a single bad entry can result in a larger percentage loss before the stop triggers. Never size a SOXL position the same way you'd size a TQQQ position.

Volatility Decay

Both ETFs reset to 3x daily and suffer from volatility decay in choppy markets. But SOXL's higher underlying volatility means it decays faster during sideways periods.

In a week where SOXX moves up 2%, down 2%, up 2%, down 2% — essentially flat — SOXL loses more value than TQQQ would under equivalent QQQ chop. The compounding math penalizes higher volatility more severely. This is why SOXL requires even more discipline around entry timing than TQQQ — entering during chop is costly in either ETF, but especially in SOXL.

The decay rule of thumb: Higher underlying volatility = faster decay in sideways markets. SOXL's decay risk is rated HIGH in the LevSig signal system for exactly this reason. Size positions accordingly and exit quickly when trend conditions break down.

Trend Behavior

When semiconductors trend, they trend hard. SOXL in a confirmed semi bull cycle can produce extraordinary returns — +100-200% moves over 3-6 months are not uncommon. The AI infrastructure buildout in 2023-2024 is a prime example: SOXX more than doubled, and SOXL produced multiples of that return for traders who caught and held the trend systematically.

TQQQ trends more steadily. The Nasdaq-100's breadth means it doesn't spike as hard in pure semiconductor cycles, but it also doesn't fall as hard when semis correct. TQQQ's trends tend to be longer, smoother, and more forgiving of imperfect entries.

For systematic traders, the choice comes down to risk tolerance:

→ SOXL — higher volatility, faster decay, bigger swings in both directions, higher potential return in semi bull cycles

→ TQQQ — lower volatility, slower decay, steadier trends, more forgiving of timing errors

Liquidity and Spreads

Both are among the most liquid leveraged ETFs available. SOXL actually has slightly higher AUM than TQQQ (~$27B vs ~$22B), which reflects its popularity among semiconductor traders. Spreads on both are typically 1-2 cents — acceptable for swing trading, though TQQQ has a slight edge in execution quality for very large position sizes.

Which One for Signal Trading?

Both are in the LevSig signal universe for good reason — they respond well to trend-following systems applied to their underlying indexes. The key differences in practice:

SOXL signals fire less frequently because semiconductors need stronger confirmation before a trend is established — the higher volatility means more noise to filter. When SOXL does fire an entry signal, the conviction is higher.

TQQQ signals are more consistent — the broader Nasdaq-100 tends to trend more cleanly and produces more signal opportunities across different market environments.

Our take: Trading both gives you the best of both worlds. TQQQ provides consistent signal opportunities in broad tech trends. SOXL adds concentrated semiconductor exposure when the AI and chip cycle is running. When both signal simultaneously it's a strong confirmation that tech as a whole is in a confirmed uptrend.

Bottom Line

SOXL and TQQQ serve different purposes in a systematic leveraged ETF portfolio. SOXL is a high-octane semiconductor play that rewards disciplined trend followers with outsized returns when semis run — and punishes holders who stay too long when the trend breaks. TQQQ is the broader, steadier tech exposure that suits most systematic traders as a core position.

Neither should be held without a systematic exit rule. Both need ATR-based trailing stops. And both deserve respect for how quickly they can move against you when conditions change.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is SOXL or TQQQ more volatile?

SOXL is significantly more volatile. Semiconductors are a concentrated, cyclical sector that moves faster and harder than the broad Nasdaq-100. SOXL routinely moves 5-10% in a single session during high-volatility periods, compared to TQQQ's typical 3-6% daily range.

Which is better for swing trading, SOXL or TQQQ?

Both are viable for systematic swing trading but suit different risk profiles. TQQQ offers broader diversification and lower decay in choppy markets. SOXL offers bigger moves when semiconductors are trending but requires tighter position sizing due to its higher volatility.

Why does SOXL drop more than TQQQ when the market falls?

SOXL tracks semiconductors — one of the most cyclical and volatile market segments. Semis are highly sensitive to earnings, supply chain news, geopolitical events, and AI demand cycles. When risk-off sentiment hits, concentrated sector ETFs like SOXL fall harder and faster than broad index ETFs like TQQQ.

Can I hold both SOXL and TQQQ at the same time?

Yes — many systematic traders hold both when both underlying indexes are in confirmed uptrends. When SOXL and TQQQ signal simultaneously, it's a strong confirmation of broad tech momentum. Be aware that both are correlated — in a broad tech selloff, both will decline simultaneously.
