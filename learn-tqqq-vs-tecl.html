The Basics

TQQQ and TECL are both 3x leveraged long ETFs focused on U.S. technology stocks. But they track different indexes, which means different stock weightings, different sector exposures, and — critically for leveraged traders — different volatility profiles.

Concentration: The Key Difference

This is where the comparison actually matters. TQQQ tracks the Nasdaq-100, which is broad enough to include Amazon, Meta, Costco, and Tesla alongside the core tech names. TECL tracks the S&P Technology Select Sector Index, which is narrower and more concentrated in Apple, Microsoft, and NVIDIA.

What does that mean in practice? TECL moves harder on mega-cap tech earnings. When Apple and Microsoft both report strong quarters, TECL outperforms TQQQ. When they miss, TECL falls further. TQQQ's broader base provides a small cushion from non-tech Nasdaq names — consumer discretionary, healthcare, and communications companies that wouldn't appear in TECL.

Liquidity and Spreads

TQQQ is the most liquid leveraged ETF in the world, with roughly 5x the assets and daily volume of TECL. For most retail traders, both are liquid enough. But if you're trading larger positions or using limit orders, TQQQ's tighter spreads matter. The difference is typically 1-2 cents per share — negligible on a $50 ETF, but it compounds across hundreds of trades.

For systematic traders: Liquidity affects how cleanly you can execute signal entries and exits. TQQQ's depth means less slippage on your stops — which is especially important in fast-moving markets where your ATR trailing stop gets hit during a gap down.

Volatility Decay

Both reset to 3x daily, so both suffer from volatility decay in sideways markets. The question is which one decays faster.

Because TECL is more concentrated, it tends to have slightly higher daily volatility — which means slightly faster decay during choppy periods. The difference isn't dramatic, but over a multi-week sideways grind, TECL can lose 2-3% more than TQQQ relative to their underlyings.

In trending markets, the reverse is true: TECL's concentration compounds harder in your favor. A clean two-month tech rally will typically produce higher returns in TECL than TQQQ because the top holdings are pulling in the same direction with less dilution from non-tech names.

Which One Trends Better for Signal Trading?

Both respond well to momentum-based trend systems. The Nasdaq-100's breadth means QQQ produces slightly more frequent trend signals — there's almost always something moving in a 100-stock index. XLK's concentration means its trends tend to be higher-conviction when they fire: when tech is going, XLK really goes.

From a practical standpoint, TQQQ is the safer default for most traders. The deeper liquidity, broader diversification, and lower decay rate in chop make it more forgiving. TECL is a better choice for traders who specifically want concentrated mega-cap tech exposure and can tolerate the wider swings.
